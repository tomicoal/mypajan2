The places

Tokyo

The way in&out
  • Haneda Airport: closer to the city centre. Easy commute to main city areas.
  • Narita Airport: Iberia and BA mainly fly here, so if you're reading this, odds are that you'll end up here. It's a quick train ride to Tokyo Station, with buses also available. Don't take a Taxi.
Moving around

Japan's public transport is the most precise in the world. Buses will be at the stop at the exact time they are scheduled. Imagine the trains...

I recommend getting a PASMO card in any mayor station. They cost around Â¥500 and will work for underground and bus lines in all mayor Japanese cities.

Since I was living as a local, I can't really tell you how to obtain the weekly-biweekly Japan Rail pass, but it is a must for anyone visiting. For the cost of a return ticket from Tokyo to Kyoto you'll get to use the whole rail network. Remember to book it in advance! Read more here.

Where to stay

Japanese hotel rooms tend to be on the small side not being uncommon to find yourself in a room where you can barely fit your luggage. In Tokyo, it's even worse. Be careful with too-budget hotels. Unless you're looking for the experience, you can end up unknowingly in a capsule room.

You'll most probably be walking or using the underground most of the day, so I don't think hotel location is the most important aspect of your Tokyo trip. Nevertheless, here are my personal recommendations.

Go to map
  • Tokyo Station - Hibiya
    • Central area of Tokyo with superb connections to the rest of the city.
    • Beautiful area that once boasted being the financial centre of Asia.
    • Direct connection to Narita and Haneda airports.
    • Close by attractions include the Imperial Palace, Tsukiji Fish market and the Artizon Museum.
  • Roppongi - Akasaka - Azabu-Juban
    • Also very centric so its easy to get around Tokyo but with worse connections to the airports.
    • Most expensive areas to live, so no surprise they're Tokyo's expat central.
    • Wide range of hotels at all price levels.
    • Close by attractions include Tokyo Tower.
  • Shibuya
    • Most visited area in the city, but that doesn't make it the best.
    • It's where you'll find the most partying so a prime location to find the infamous salaryman passed out.
    • If you're into luxury shopping, close by Omotesando is the place to go.
    • Fun area to visit, but can't really recommend staying.
    • Close by attractions include Shibuya crossing, Meiji Park and Harajuku.
  • Shinjuku
    • The area where you'll find the cheapest stay, but at a price. Although crime is basically non-existent in Japan, it is by far the "dodgiest" area in Tokyo.
    • Super fun to go out for a stroll when the sun goes down.
    • Close by attractions include Golden Gai and Shinjuku Gyoen Garden.
  • Asakusa
    • Tokyo's "old town".
    • Beautiful area with plenty of traditional japanese temples.
    • Close by attractions include Senso-Ji temple and Tokyo Skytree.
Where to eat

This may come as a shock, but Japan is slowing transforming into a modern economy, so expect many cash only establishments.

  • As a rule, anything above a 3.8 in Google Maps will be good, but here are a few of my favourites:
    • SG LOW: Japanese Izakaya with a modern twist.
    • Shirubee Shibuya: run down izakaya with excellent food.
    • Andy's Shin Hinomoto: authentic izakaya underneath the train tracks.
    • Ramen Nujaki: all ramen shops are good but here is an example.
    • Gonpachi: Kill bill fan? Place to go. Food is actually good and cheap for a tourist trap.
    • Misakiko: cheap rotating sushi brought to you by train.
    • Gyozaro: visiting Harajuku? It's gyoza o'clock.
    • Tsukiji Fish market: only for lunch, plenty of food stalls to choose from.

While at an Izakaya, don't forget to ask if they have nomihodai. Usually around Â¥1,500-Â¥3,000 per person all you can drink experience. Even good places can have it. A beer can go for as much as Â¥850 so...worth it.

Remember, no tipping. If you leave a few coins on the table, waiters will chase you down the street to give them back.

Kamakura

Temple central

Just one-hour train ride south of Tokyo you'll find Kamakura, once Japan's medieval capital. I don't necessarily recommend staying at Kamakura. One day is plenty of time to see it.

Hopping-off the train at Kita-Kamakura and walking down to the town is the way to go. The temples bellow are in order if you choose to do so.

What to visit
  • There's hundred of different shrines in and around the city. Here my favourites:
    • Engaku-ji: huge zen buddhist temple.
    • Hokokuji Temple: within you'll find a bamboo forest.
    • Hokokuji Temple: within you'll find a bamboo forest.
    • Kamakura Hasedera: bit of a climb, but worth the views.
    • Kotoku-in: giant Buddha.
Where to eat
  • While visiting Kamakura you mainly have 2 options:
    • Komachi-dori is the main food hub in the city.
    • head down to the beach to any of the seaside restaurants.

Nikko

Getting to Nikko takes a bit longer. It's a 2hour ride from Tokyo Station. Depending on your schedule I recommend staying the night by lake Chuzenji and trying out one of the traditional Japanese Ryokan hotels.

What to visit
  • There's hundred of different shrines in and around the city. Here my favourites:
    • Nikko Toshogu: shrine dedicated to the first Shogun.
    • Kanmangafuchi Abyss: walk by the river surrounded by nature.
    • Takino'o Shrine: escape the crowds and head into the forest.

Mount Fuji

No intro needed

For the best photos, head to Chureito Pagoda. Mount Fuji always has a cloud on top of it, so consider yourself lucky if you get to see the top.

Lake Hakone

A good place to enjoy a stay at a Ryokan, Japan's traditional hotels. These hotels normally include dinner service, which will be served in your room. Also, forget about beds, you'll be sleeping on the floor.

Kyoto

The old Capital
The way in&out
  • Train: Shinkansen from tokyo every 30 minutes. Easiest and fastest way to get there.
  • Flying: closest airport is in Osaka, with daily flights from Tokyo starting around Â¥10,000.
Where to stay

Although Kyoto is relatively big, I only recommend staying around the Gion neighbourhood and the immediate surroundings. The rest of the city has little to offer.

Moving around

Again, Google Maps is here to the rescue. Bus times are extremely precise and the best way to move around the city.

What to visit
  • Much like Kamakura, Kyoto is packed with temples to visit. Here a few of my favorites:
    • Kiyomizu-dera: Buddhist temple with scenic views.
    • Sanneizaka: pedestrian street surrounded by traditional japanese architecture.
    • Chion-in Temple: impresive Buddhist temple.
    • Bamboo forest: bit of a letdown if you ask me, but everyone seems to like it.
    • Kinkaku-ji: the golden temple.
    • Fushimi Inari Taisha: fox temple with hundreds of tori-gate path.
    • Imperial Palace: the old imperial epicentre.
    • Nijo Castle: the real seat of power.
Where to eat
  • Much like Kamakura, Kyoto is packed with temples to visit. Here a few of my favorites:
    • Enen: surprisingly good yakinikyu restaurant tucked in between old wooden houses.

Worth the visit

Takayama and Shirakawa-go

Staying at Takayama and visiting Shirakawa provides a unique insight into local japanese living and traditional japanese house styles. It's a bit of a detour, so I only recommend it if you have the time to spare. Although getting to Takayama is tricky, buses are available from Takayama to Shirakawa.

What to visit
  • Some recommended stops:
    • Takayama's historical houses
    • Sake brewery
    • Sake brewery
    • Shirakawa opbservation deck: best views of the town.

Osaka and Nara

Japan's food mecca, Osaka is a worth-it stop. On the way to Nara from Kyoto so it isn't too much of a detour. Don't forget to wait for the deer to bow before feeding them!

What to visit
    • Osaka castle.
    • Dotonbori: Lose yourself in the endless restaurants.
    • Todaji: mid-700s buddhist temple with Japan's largest Buddha.

Himeji Castle

The iconic Himeji Castle stands on your way from Osaka to Hiroshima. If you're planning to go that far south, think about giving it a visit. It's one of the few places I regret not going to.

Hiroshima - Miyajima

Little has to be said about Hiroshima. Miyajima is beautiful island just off the coast of Hiroshima. You can get there by boat or train.

What to visit
    • Peace Memorial.
    • Atomic bomb dome
    • Itsukushima Jinja: orange shinto gate that floats at high tide.
